# Bills of Exchange and Accounting Entries

(Teacher Mode – Practical + Banking Oriented)

------------------------------------------------------------

# 1. What is a Bill of Exchange?

A Bill of Exchange is:

A written instrument
Containing an unconditional order
Signed by the drawer
Directing the drawee
To pay a certain sum
On a specified date
To a specified person.

In simple words:

It is a written promise to pay money in future.

------------------------------------------------------------

# 2. Parties to a Bill

There are three main parties:

1. Drawer – Person who draws the bill (creditor)
2. Drawee – Person who accepts and pays (debtor)
3. Payee – Person who receives payment

Sometimes:
Drawer and Payee can be same.

------------------------------------------------------------

# 3. Why Are Bills Used?

Bills are used in:

- Credit sales
- Trade transactions
- Business payments
- Banking discounting

They provide:

- Legal security
- Fixed due date
- Negotiability

------------------------------------------------------------

# 4. Types of Instruments of Credit

Common credit instruments:

- Bill of Exchange
- Promissory Note
- Cheque

------------------------------------------------------------

## Difference Between Bill and Promissory Note

Bill of Exchange:
- Order to pay
- 3 parties

Promissory Note:
- Promise to pay
- 2 parties

------------------------------------------------------------

# 5. Term and Due Date of a Bill

Bill may be:

- Sight Bill (payable on demand)
- Time Bill (payable after certain period)

------------------------------------------------------------

## Calculating Due Date

If bill is for 3 months:

Add 3 months to acceptance date
Add 3 days of grace

Example:

Bill dated 1 January
3 months bill

Add 3 months → 1 April
Add 3 days → 4 April (Due date)

------------------------------------------------------------

# 6. Important Terms

- Acceptance: Drawee signs bill agreeing to pay.
- Maturity Date: Final date for payment.
- Endorsement: Transfer of bill to another person.
- Discounting: Selling bill to bank before maturity.

------------------------------------------------------------

# 7. Accounting Entries (Very Important)

Let us understand through example.

------------------------------------------------------------

## Example:

A sells goods to B for ₹50,000 on credit.
B accepts bill for 3 months.

------------------------------------------------------------

### (1) At time of sale:

B A/c Dr 50,000  
To Sales A/c 50,000

------------------------------------------------------------

### (2) On acceptance of bill:

Bills Receivable A/c Dr 50,000  
To B A/c 50,000

Now debtor is replaced by Bill.

------------------------------------------------------------

### (3) On maturity (payment received):

Cash A/c Dr 50,000  
To Bills Receivable A/c 50,000

------------------------------------------------------------

# 8. Discounting of Bill

If A goes to bank and discounts bill before maturity:

Bank deducts discount and gives balance.

------------------------------------------------------------

### Example:

Bill ₹50,000
Discount ₹2,000

Entry:

Bank A/c Dr 48,000  
Discount A/c Dr 2,000  
To Bills Receivable A/c 50,000

Discount is expense.

------------------------------------------------------------

# 9. Dishonour of Bill

If drawee fails to pay:

------------------------------------------------------------

### Entry:

B A/c Dr 50,000  
To Bills Receivable A/c 50,000

Debtor comes back in books.

If noting charges paid:

B A/c Dr  
To Cash A/c

------------------------------------------------------------

# 10. Accommodation Bill

Accommodation bill is:

A bill drawn without actual trade.
It is drawn to help each other financially.

Used to:

- Raise temporary funds
- Discount with bank

It does not represent real sale.

------------------------------------------------------------

# 11. Bill Books

Businesses maintain:

- Bills Receivable Book
- Bills Payable Book

This helps:

- Tracking maturity
- Monitoring cash flow
- Avoiding dishonour

------------------------------------------------------------

# 12. Importance in Banking

Banks deal with:

- Bill discounting
- Trade finance
- Documentary bills
- Inland and foreign bills

Bills are important in:

- MSME financing
- Working capital finance
- Export credit

------------------------------------------------------------

# 13. Common Beginner Mistakes

- Forgetting 3 days of grace
- Confusing discount with commission
- Not reversing entry on dishonour
- Treating bill as revenue

------------------------------------------------------------

# Quick Revision Points

- Bill = order to pay
- 3 parties
- 3 days grace
- Discounting = bank gives cash early
- Dishonour brings debtor back
