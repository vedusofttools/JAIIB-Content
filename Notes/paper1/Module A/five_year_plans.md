# Five Year Plans in India 

## Introduction
Five Year Plans were the main instruments of **economic planning in India** after Independence.  
They aimed at **planned economic development** by setting targets, priorities, and resource allocation for a fixed period of five years.

India followed this planning model under the guidance of the **:contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}**, which was established in **1950**.

---

## Objectives of Five Year Plans
- Rapid economic growth
- Balanced regional development
- Reduction of poverty and unemployment
- Self-reliance
- Social justice and equity
- Efficient utilisation of resources

---

## Background of Planning in India
- India became independent in **1947**
- Faced problems of poverty, low growth, unemployment
- Adopted a **mixed economy model**
- Planning was considered essential for development

---

## Classification of Plans in India
- Five Year Plans
- Annual Plans
- Rolling Plans
- Plan holidays (gap periods)

---

## First Five Year Plan (1951–1956)

## Focus
- Agriculture and irrigation
- Rehabilitation after Partition

## Key Features
- Emphasis on food production
- Development of irrigation projects
- Price stability

## Important Projects
- Bhakra Nangal Dam
- Hirakud Dam
- Damodar Valley Project

## Outcome
- Successful
- Agricultural growth exceeded targets

---

## Second Five Year Plan (1956–1961)

## Focus
- Industrialisation
- Heavy industries

## Model Used
- Mahalanobis Model

## Key Features
- Expansion of public sector
- Development of steel and capital goods industries

## Outcome
- Industrial growth
- Neglect of agriculture led to food shortages

---

## Third Five Year Plan (1961–1966)

## Focus
- Balanced growth of agriculture and industry

## Problems Faced
- Indo-China War (1962)
- Indo-Pak War (1965)
- Severe droughts

## Outcome
- Failure
- Inflation and food shortages

---

## Plan Holiday (1966–1969)
- No Five Year Plans
- Followed **Annual Plans**
- Due to economic instability

---

## Fourth Five Year Plan (1969–1974)

## Focus
- Growth with stability
- Self-reliance

## Key Features
- Nationalisation of banks (1969)
- Focus on poverty alleviation

## Outcome
- Mixed results
- Slow growth

---

## Fifth Five Year Plan (1974–1979)

## Focus
- Poverty eradication
- Employment generation

## Slogan
- Garibi Hatao

## Outcome
- Terminated early in **1978**

---

## Rolling Plan (1978–1980)
- Introduced by Janata Government
- Replaced fixed five-year planning temporarily
- Discontinued later

---

## Sixth Five Year Plan (1980–1985)

## Focus
- Economic liberalisation (initial phase)
- Poverty reduction

## Key Features
- Expansion of public sector
- Growth with social justice

## Outcome
- Reasonable success
- Economic growth improved

---

## Seventh Five Year Plan (1985–1990)

## Focus
- Productivity and efficiency
- Technological modernisation

## Key Features
- Emphasis on private sector
- Employment generation

## Outcome
- High growth rate
- Rise in fiscal deficit

---

## Plan Holiday (1990–1992)
- Political and economic instability
- Balance of Payments crisis

---

## Eighth Five Year Plan (1992–1997)

## Focus
- Liberalisation, Privatisation, Globalisation (LPG)

## Key Features
- Market-oriented reforms
- Reduced role of public sector
- Encouragement to private and foreign investment

## Outcome
- High economic growth
- Integration with global economy

---

## Ninth Five Year Plan (1997–2002)

## Focus
- Growth with social justice

## Key Features
- Poverty reduction
- Human development

## Outcome
- Moderate growth
- Mixed performance

---

## Tenth Five Year Plan (2002–2007)

## Focus
- High growth with equity

## Key Features
- Targeted 8% GDP growth
- Employment generation

## Outcome
- GDP growth achieved close to target

---

## Eleventh Five Year Plan (2007–2012)

## Focus
- Inclusive growth

## Key Features
- Education
- Health
- Skill development

## Outcome
- Good social sector focus
- Impact of global financial crisis

---

## Twelfth Five Year Plan (2012–2017)

## Focus
- Faster, sustainable and more inclusive growth

## Key Features
- Sustainability
- Infrastructure
- Skill development

## Outcome
- Last Five Year Plan
- Moderate success

---

## End of Five Year Plans
- Five Year Plans ended in **2017**
- Planning Commission replaced by **NITI Aayog (2015)**
- Shift from rigid planning to flexible, outcome-based planning

---

## Role of Five Year Plans in Indian Economy
- Built industrial base
- Improved agriculture and infrastructure
- Reduced poverty
- Promoted self-reliance
- Strengthened public sector

---

## Limitations of Five Year Plans
- Over-centralisation
- Inefficient implementation
- Regional imbalances
- Excessive government control
- Fiscal deficits

---

## Five Year Plans and Banking
- Directed credit programmes
- Priority Sector Lending
- Bank nationalisation
- Expansion of rural banking

Banking sector reforms evolved gradually from planning era to market-based system.
