# Union Budget: Receipts, Expenditure, Plan Expenditure and Deficit Concepts

## Union Budget – Meaning
The Union Budget is the **annual financial statement** of the Government of India that presents:
- Estimated receipts
- Estimated expenditure

for a financial year (1 April to 31 March).

It reflects the **government’s fiscal policy**, economic priorities, and development strategy.

---

## Constitutional Basis
The Union Budget is presented under:
- Article 112 of the Constitution of India

It must be approved by Parliament before implementation.

---

## Structure of the Union Budget
The Union Budget consists of:
- Revenue Budget
- Capital Budget

Both receipts and expenditure are classified into **revenue** and **capital** components.

---

## Budget Receipts

## Meaning
Budget receipts are the **inflows of funds** to the government during a financial year.

Receipts are classified into:
- Revenue receipts
- Capital receipts

---

## Revenue Receipts

## Meaning
Revenue receipts are those receipts which:
- Do not create any liability, and
- Do not reduce government assets

They are **recurring in nature**.

---

## Components of Revenue Receipts

### Tax Revenue
Revenue earned through taxes.

Includes:
- Direct taxes (income tax, corporate tax)
- Indirect taxes (GST, customs duty, excise duty)

---

### Non-Tax Revenue
Revenue earned from sources other than taxes.

Includes:
- Interest receipts
- Dividends from public sector enterprises
- Fees, fines, penalties
- Profits of government enterprises

---

## Capital Receipts

## Meaning
Capital receipts are those receipts which:
- Create a liability, or
- Reduce government assets

They are **non-recurring or long-term** in nature.

---

## Components of Capital Receipts

### Borrowings
- Market borrowings
- Loans from financial institutions
- Small savings

Borrowings create **future repayment obligations**.

---

### Recovery of Loans
- Repayment of loans given earlier by government

This reduces government assets.

---

### Disinvestment
- Sale of government equity in public sector enterprises

Reduces government ownership.

---

## Budget Expenditure

## Meaning
Budget expenditure refers to the **outflow of funds** by the government during a financial year.

Expenditure is classified into:
- Revenue expenditure
- Capital expenditure

---

## Revenue Expenditure

## Meaning
Revenue expenditure is expenditure which:
- Does not create assets, and
- Does not reduce liabilities

It is **recurring in nature**.

---

## Examples of Revenue Expenditure
- Salaries and pensions
- Interest payments
- Subsidies
- Defence revenue expenditure
- Administrative expenses

Revenue expenditure supports **day-to-day functioning** of government.

---

## Capital Expenditure

## Meaning
Capital expenditure is expenditure which:
- Creates assets, or
- Reduces liabilities

It contributes to **long-term growth**.

---

## Examples of Capital Expenditure
- Infrastructure development
- Roads, railways, ports
- Capital outlay on defence
- Loan repayment
- Investment in public sector enterprises

Capital expenditure has a **multiplier effect** on the economy.

---

## Plan Expenditure and Non-Plan Expenditure (Conceptual)

## Plan Expenditure
Plan expenditure refers to expenditure incurred on:
- Developmental schemes
- Projects outlined in economic planning

It includes:
- Central sector schemes
- Centrally sponsored schemes

---

## Non-Plan Expenditure
Non-plan expenditure refers to:
- Expenditure on routine and committed items

Includes:
- Salaries
- Pensions
- Interest payments
- Defence expenditure

---

## Important Note (Exam-Relevant)
- The Plan vs Non-Plan classification was **discontinued from 2017-18**
- Now expenditure is classified as:
    - Revenue expenditure
    - Capital expenditure

However, **conceptual questions may still refer to Plan expenditure**.

---

## Deficit Concepts

Deficits show the **gap between government receipts and expenditure**.

They indicate the **fiscal health** of the economy.

---

## Revenue Deficit

## Meaning
Revenue Deficit = Revenue Expenditure – Revenue Receipts

It indicates:
- Government is borrowing to meet routine expenses
- Poor quality of expenditure

Revenue deficit is considered **undesirable**.

---

## Fiscal Deficit

## Meaning
Fiscal Deficit = Total Expenditure – (Revenue Receipts + Non-Debt Capital Receipts)

It represents:
- Total borrowing requirement of the government

Fiscal deficit is the **most important deficit indicator**.

---

## Budget Deficit
Budget Deficit = Total Expenditure – Total Receipts

This concept is **no longer used** for policy analysis.

---

## Primary Deficit

## Meaning
Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit – Interest Payments

It shows:
- Fiscal imbalance excluding interest burden
- Current government’s borrowing impact

---

## Monetised Deficit

## Meaning
Monetised deficit refers to:
- Financing fiscal deficit through creation of new money

It is inflationary in nature.

---

## Importance of Deficit Concepts

- Indicate fiscal discipline
- Affect inflation
- Influence interest rates
- Impact public debt
- Affect economic stability

---

## Union Budget and Banking Sector
- Fiscal deficit affects interest rates
- Government borrowing influences liquidity
- Capital expenditure boosts credit demand
- Tax policies affect savings and investment
- Budget impacts banking business directly

---

## Common Exam Traps
- Confusing revenue deficit with fiscal deficit
- Treating borrowings as revenue receipts
- Assuming plan expenditure still exists
- Ignoring interest payments in primary deficit
